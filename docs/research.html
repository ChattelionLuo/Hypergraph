Research

Graph and hypergraph learning for statistical ranking.

A mathematical introduction to the objects behind Hypergraph: graphs, hypergraphs, incidence structure, Laplacian regularization, and probabilistic ranking models on relational data.

Graphs

Pairwise relations as operators.

A weighted graph is \(G=(V,E,W)\), where \(V=\{1,\ldots,n\}\) is a finite vertex set, \(E\subseteq V\times V\) is a set of pairwise relations, and \(W=(w_{ij})\) stores edge weights. In ranking applications, a vertex may represent a player, asset, or item, while an edge records observed comparison information.

The adjacency matrix \(A\) and degree matrix \(D=\mathrm{diag}(d_1,\ldots,d_n)\), with \(d_i=\sum_j A_{ij}\), define the graph Laplacian \[ L = D-A. \] For a signal \(f:V\to\mathbb{R}\), the quadratic form \[ f^\top L f = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j} A_{ij}(f_i-f_j)^2 \] penalizes rapid variation across strongly connected vertices. This is the basic mechanism behind graph smoothing, spectral embeddings, semi-supervised learning, and regularized ranking.

Hypergraphs

Beyond pairwise edges.

A hypergraph is \( \mathcal{H}=(V,\mathcal{E},\omega) \), where each hyperedge \(e\in\mathcal{E}\) is a subset of vertices rather than a pair. The incidence matrix \(H\in\{0,1\}^{|V|\times|\mathcal{E}|}\) is \[ H(v,e)=\mathbf{1}\{v\in e\}. \] Hyperedges encode higher-order interactions: a match with contextual covariates, a race field, a market basket, or a group event can be represented as one relational object rather than a collection of independent pairwise edges.

With vertex degrees \(d(v)=\sum_{e}\omega(e)H(v,e)\) and hyperedge degrees \(\delta(e)=\sum_v H(v,e)\), a normalized hypergraph Laplacian can be written as \[ \Delta_{\mathcal{H}} = I - D_v^{-1/2} H W_e D_e^{-1} H^\top D_v^{-1/2}. \] The induced regularizer favors functions that vary smoothly within informative hyperedges, while still allowing sharp separation where the data support it.

Statistical learning

Prediction as structured risk minimization.

Let \(x_i\) be item-level or event-level covariates, and let \(s_\theta(i)\) denote a latent score produced by a parametric model. A graph-regularized estimator solves a problem of the form \[ \widehat{\theta} \in \arg\min_{\theta} \left\{ \frac{1}{m}\sum_{\ell=1}^{m}\ell\big(y_\ell, p_\theta(\ell)\big) + \lambda\, s_\theta^\top L s_\theta \right\}, \] where the loss may be negative log-likelihood, Brier loss, or another proper scoring rule.

Hypergraph learning replaces \(L\) by a higher-order operator \(\Delta_{\mathcal{H}}\), or constructs neural/message-passing layers over \(H\). In both cases, the statistical goal is the same: borrow information across related observations while controlling variance, calibration, and out-of-sample risk.

Ranking models

From latent scores to event probabilities.

Pairwise ranking starts from a latent strength difference. In a Bradley-Terry-style model, \[ \mathbb{P}(i \succ j) = \frac{\exp(s_i)}{\exp(s_i)+\exp(s_j)} = \sigma(s_i-s_j). \] Modern ranking systems extend this idea by allowing scores to depend on context, time, covariates, graph neighborhoods, and higher-order event structure.

The practical object published by Hypergraph is not a betting prescription. It is a calibrated probability statement \(p_\theta(y=1\mid \mathcal{F}_t)\) conditional on the information set available before an event. The public demo is designed for research communication, model comparison, and product illustration; it is not gambling advice or a betting strategy.

ATP demo study

21,385 matches, 389 players, and a held-out 2024-2025 test window. The demo highlights probability calibration and ranking performance without disclosing proprietary implementation details.
